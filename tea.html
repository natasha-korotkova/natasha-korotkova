May 8: Lisa Bylinina (Utrecht Institute of Linguistics OTS)

Semantics of subjectivity: 'tasty', 'fun' and beyond

Proper semantic treatment of statements whose truth is a matter of opinion ("The cake is tasty") rather than a matter of fact ("John was born in 1983") has been a widely discussed topic especially after Lasersohn's influential work in 2005. There are several theories on the market that cover the basic cases of judge-dependency, but extending them to more classes of subjective predicates faces the problem of the heterogeneity of such predicates. As one example, I focus on 'judge phrases' that some subjective predicates take while others don't ("The ride was fun for John" vs. "*The student was lazy for John") and observe that these PPs appear when the predicate makes reference to a non-externalizable experience event. I propose that a baseline theory of judge-dependency à la Lasersohn (2005) equipped with one preliminary restriction on the judge value (judge = experiencer) accounts for the data. I take it as a case that could motivate a more systematic study of the conditions under which the judge can or has to be shifted, hopefully relating it to what is known about indexical shifting phenomena in general.

June 5: Walter Pedersen (McGill)

A scalar analysis of 'again'

This paper explores the thesis that, despite their appellation, 'adverbs of repetition' – like English again – do not actually encode a concept of repetition. Instead, it is proposed that they encode an abstract concept of negative scalar change. This proposal not only accords with recent scalar accounts of verb-meaning (e.g. Winter 2006; Kennedy and Levin 2008), it also avoids the downfalls of analyses of adverbial-ambiguities that are based in a traditional decompositional framework (e.g. von Stechow 1996; Beck 2005), without sacrificing the empirical coverage offered by these approaches. Moreover, the scalar analysis offered here can provide a principled explanation of cross-linguistic variation in the availability of restitutive readings, and also shed light on why adverbs like almost and again give rise to ambiguities, while other adverbs (often, never) do not.

March 4, 2013: Daniel Altshuler, on neutral aspect  [slides]

There is no neutral aspect

Carlota Smith (1991) proposed the notion of "neutral aspect" in order to classify aspectual forms in many of the world's languages that appear to have semantic properties of both the perfective and the imperfective. This notion has played a vital role in many analyses of aspect (e.g. Iatridou et al. 2001, Giorgi & Pianesi 2001, Pancheva 2003, Boneh & Doron 2008, Travis 2010). I argue that we can maintain a traditional perfective/imperfective distinction -- without reference to neutrality -- if we distinguish boundedness from telicity (e.g. Verkuyl 1993, Depreaetere 1995, Borik 2006). In particular, I argue that a form is semantically perfective if and only if it encodes a Boundedness Requirement: a VP-event part must either have culminated or not have developed any further (cf. Krifka 1989, Koenig & Muansuwan 2000). This requirement is made precise by extending Landman's (1992) analysis of the English progressive. The proposed analysis predicts a typology of partitive (im)perfective operators, i.e. ones that lead to the imperfective paradox (Dowty 1979), according to which the operators differ not only in whether they encode the Boundedness Requirement, but also whether they require proper event parts in the extension of the VP that they combine with. I show how this latter parameter explains Smith’s (1991) observation that -- in contrast to progressive of achievements -- neutral aspect of achievements doesn't lead to coercion.

February 4, 2013: Barbara Maria Tomaszewicz, USC, on superlatives [handout]

Comparison of Degrees or Individuals? Focus Effects in Superlatives in Polish

Superlative expressions are evaluated relative to a comparison class. Heim 1999 introduced two lexical entries for -est with truth-conditionally equivalent meaning, a 3-place -est on which individuals are compared, and a 2-place -est comparing degree sets. The question of what the right meaning for -est is is still debated (e.g., Romero 2011, Szabolcsi 2012). I provide a syntactic argument from degree relative clauses that the availability of the 2-place vs. 3-place semantics for -est in Polish is constrained by the presence of focus. I conclude that the 2-place -est is required for modification by degree relative clauses (Howard 2012), while the 3-place -est is required for focus association. Focus association is necessary for relative readings, since the requirement that -est associates with focus can explain the unavailability of degree relative clauses with relative superlatives in Polish.
