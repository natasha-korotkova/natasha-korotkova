welcome note

I am a PhD candidate at the UCLA Linguistics Department. Before coming to UCLA, I studied linguistics at the Russian State University for the Humanities in Moscow, from where I graduated in 2009 with an equivalent of Master's degree.

My main research interests are formal semantics, its interfaces with syntax, and cross-linguistic variation within these domains. The empirical phenomena I study from this angle are evidentiality, attitude predicates, modality and parenthesis. My dissertation encompasses several projects on the interaction of evidentials and attitudes across languages. For more information, see my dissertation prospectus and the corresponding section here.

In addition to a more general typological perspective, I am also interested in micro-variation in geographically and/or genetically related languages. This interest of mine currently manifests itself in two projects. One is the typology of wh-scope marking and similar-looking phenomena in Slavic. Another is on evidential perfects in Georgian, Bulgarian and Turkish.

I have done fieldwork on a variety of languages (Adyghe, Georgian, Imbabura Quichua, Kalmyk, Khakas, Turkish). My first fieldwork experience is work on the morphology and semantics of tense and aspect system of Adyghe, a polysynthetic language Adyghe spoken in the Caucasus. I am still a part of the language documentation project on Northwest Caucasian languages, hosted by the Russian State University for the Humanities.

Each of the projects above is described in greater detail here. The CV contains the full list of things I've been up to during my linguistics career.

In my view, linguistics ought to be data-driven. It means that traditional informal elicitation should be, whenever possible, complemented with other techiques of accessing grammaticality/felicity. I implement this view in my research. In work with understudied languages, it translates into using alternative methodologies such as storyboards, and into text collection and corpus building. In work with well-documented and accessible languages like English or Russian, it means using data sets obtained from corpora, through experiments and via online questionnaires, e.g. Amazon Mechanical Turk.
